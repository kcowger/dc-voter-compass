# DC Council At-Large (Democratic Primary)

## Race overview

DC has four At-Large councilmembers who represent the entire city. Anita Bonds has held one of these seats for 13 years and is not running again. Bonds was a moderate Democrat and often the deciding vote in 2-vote margin debates between progressive and centrist members, more often siding with centrists.

This race is the highest-stakes ideological inflection point on the 2026 ballot. Whoever wins could nudge the council in either direction.

**Ranked-choice voting applies.** Voters can rank multiple candidates. This is the first time RCV will affect this race; previously Bonds survived primaries with as little as 35% of the vote when multiple challengers split the field.

## Candidates

Nine candidates qualified for the Democratic primary ballot.

## Kevin Chavous

**Age**: 41
**Neighborhood**: Ward 7 (east of the river)
**Current role**: Director of the Committee on Executive Administration and Labor, chaired by Anita Bonds
**Background**: Lawyer (Howard Law). Son of former Ward 7 Councilmember Kevin P. Chavous (1990s to 2005). Worked at Housing Counseling Services on TOPA tenant organizing.

### Stated priorities

- Education and youth services
- Inclusionary zoning expansion
- Housing affordability

### Specific positions

- Wants a counselor and nurse in every school regardless of enrollment
- Wants to fund out-of-school time programming
- Wants to raise inclusionary zoning requirements closer to 15% of total residential floor area (from current 8-10%)
- Views the at-large role as "listening and being a conduit from the community to the legislation"

### Endorsements

- **Anita Bonds** (the retiring incumbent he would replace)

### Inferred profile

- **Ideological lean (inferred)**: Moderate. The Bonds endorsement is the strongest possible signal, as Chavous worked directly for her and she explicitly chose him as the continuation candidate.
- **Background type**: Government insider (council committee director); community-rooted (Ward 7)

### Strengths

- Currently runs a council committee; lowest learning curve
- Lifelong DC resident in an underrepresented part of the city
- Direct line into housing law via TOPA experience
- Specific concrete proposal on inclusionary zoning

### Weaknesses to flag

- The Bonds endorsement marks him as continuity with the moderate swing-vote politics that progressive voters may want to replace
- Raising inclusionary zoning requirements is controversial; some housing economists argue higher IZ requirements reduce overall production
- The "conduit" framing is less aggressive than other candidates' theories of the office

## Dyana Forester

**Age**: 46
**Neighborhood**: Southeast DC (grew up there)
**Current role**: Senior director of labor relations to Maryland Governor Wes Moore
**Background**: Started as a community organizer at Teaching for Change. Worked at the DC chapter of Jobs with Justice. Was political and community affairs director for UFCW Local 400. Elected president of the AFL-CIO's Metropolitan Washington Council. Became a mom at 18. Former commissioner at DC Housing Authority.

### Stated priorities

- Working families and budget priorities aligned with DC values
- Affordable housing for families
- Worker protections and labor

### Specific positions

- Worked on successful legislation to increase the minimum wage and implement paid family leave in DC at UFCW
- Interested in workforce housing for teachers, firefighters, bus drivers, and police
- **Explicitly pushed back on "build faster" as the first housing priority**, asking whether building faster is the right solution to create affordable housing for families
- Says she doesn't want to be a councilmember forever; views the role as service

### Endorsements

- Some unions (specific list not in source material)

### Inferred profile

- **Ideological lean (inferred)**: Progressive. Labor leader career, working class lived experience, current role under progressive Maryland Gov. Wes Moore.
- **Background type**: Labor and government; partial insider via Maryland role and DC Housing Authority commissioner experience

### Strengths

- Strongest working-class lived experience in the field
- Real legislative track record on minimum wage and paid family leave through UFCW work
- Authentic east-of-the-river roots (grew up in Southeast)
- Clear values framework around budget tradeoffs

### Weaknesses to flag

- **Housing supply skeptic**: Her published quote questioning whether building faster is the right solution is a real flag for voters who consider supply expansion a top priority
- Currently works in Maryland government, not DC; some voters may want a candidate already inside DC's system
- Single-term plan may limit ability to build seniority

## Fred Hill

**Age**: 57
**Neighborhood**: DC (specific neighborhood not in source)
**Current role**: Federal contractor (ran his own firm for nearly 30 years); recently stepped down from BZA
**Background**: 10 years as chairman of the DC Board of Zoning Adjustment (BZA). Grew up in rural Poolesville, Maryland. Father is Native American; mother immigrated from Sikkim.

### Stated priorities

- Housing (fast-tracking)
- Small business and local job growth
- Strategic rather than activist approach to federal interference

### Specific positions

- Fast-track any affordable housing project that gets city financing so it isn't delayed in bureaucracy
- Argues the council needs at least one businessperson, since "almost all of them came from inside the system"
- Cites lessons learned from being bullied as a child: "when they are bigger than you are, you have to work the system to be able to survive and get your way" — not an activist approach
- Supports Kenyan McDuffie for mayor

### Endorsements

Not specifically documented in source material.

### Inferred profile

- **Ideological lean (inferred)**: Moderate. Business-friendly framing, support for McDuffie (the moderate mayoral candidate), explicit anti-activist self-description.
- **Background type**: Private sector and quasi-government via BZA chair role

### Strengths

- Deepest zoning expertise in the field (10 years on BZA)
- Genuine business operator perspective; underrepresented on current council
- Concrete proposal on fast-tracking affordable projects

### Weaknesses to flag

- Self-identified moderate may not appeal to voters seeking to replace Bonds with a more progressive voice
- McDuffie endorsement signals broader political alignment
- "Not an activist" stance may not match the moment if voters want more aggressive federal pushback

## Candace Tiana Nelson

**Age**: 50
**Neighborhood**: Brightwood (Ward 4)
**Current role**: Most recently chief of staff to Ward 4 Councilmember Janeese Lewis George
**Background**: 20 years across five DC government agencies under various mayors. Led Attorney General Brian Schwalb's transition into office in 2022.

### Stated priorities

- Council oversight of executive agencies
- Education, healthcare, housing as foundations
- Federal affairs coordination

### Specific positions

- Argues current council oversight is "performative"; criticizes that not all councilmembers show up to hearings
- Wants the council to have its own office for federal affairs (rather than the current setup with one person in Chairman Mendelson's office)
- Wants a standalone committee on education
- Wants to bring back a committee focused exclusively on finance and revenue
- Wants council hearings scheduled at times and places more convenient for regular people
- Argues good oversight of existing housing dollars could get more out of the money DC already spends

### Endorsements

Not specifically documented in source material.

### Inferred profile

- **Ideological lean (inferred)**: Progressive. Served as chief of staff to Janeese Lewis George, one of the most progressive members of the council. Note: she has also worked under multiple mayors of varying ideology over 20 years, so the JLG affiliation is the strongest recent signal.
- **Background type**: Deep government insider (5 agencies, council COS, AG transition lead)

### Strengths

- Deepest insider profile in the field by a clear margin
- Oversight is her signature issue and core competency, not a talking point
- Specific structural proposals (committees, hearings, federal affairs office)
- Strong progressive credential via JLG affiliation

### Weaknesses to flag

- Housing is in her stated priorities but supply expansion is not her primary framing; her angle is oversight of existing housing spending
- Behind-the-scenes career means limited public voting record or independent policy stances on file
- Brightwood location means less direct east-of-the-river lived experience compared to several other candidates

## Framework dimensions for this race

### Dimension 1: Ideological direction for the seat

Bonds was the moderate swing vote. Replacing her affects council balance.

- A) Shift the seat progressive
- B) Keep the centrist balance
- C) Ideology matters less than competence

### Dimension 2: Most important issue area

- A) Housing supply and affordability
- B) Public safety and crime
- C) Education quality and equity
- D) Government oversight, accountability, budget discipline
- E) Federal resilience, DC autonomy, economic stabilization

### Dimension 3: Candidate profile preference

- A) Deep policy expert in one domain
- B) Government insider who knows how DC agencies work
- C) Community-rooted advocate from an underrepresented area
- D) Private-sector or outsider perspective

### Candidate fit matrix

| Candidate | Best on Dim 1 | Best on Dim 2 | Best on Dim 3 |
|-----------|---------------|---------------|---------------|
| Chavous | B | A, C | B + C |
| Davis | C (no strong tells) | C | A |
| Forester | A | A (with affordability focus, supply-skeptical) | C |
| Hill | B | A (fast-track) | D |
| Jackson | C | B | B (partial) |
| Jenkins | A (probable) | C | C |
| Nelson | A | D (dominant) | B (dominant) |
| Owolewa | A (dominant) | E | C |
| Raymond | C | A (dominant for supply) | A + B |

**Reading the matrix**: A candidate appearing in a column means they are a strong fit on that dimension. Multiple letters means they fit multiple sub-options within that dimension.

### Cross-dimensional standouts

For voters who prioritize **progressive + insider + oversight**: Nelson is the dominant choice.
For voters who prioritize **progressive + housing supply**: Raymond on supply, but Nelson on the progressive dimension; this is a tradeoff.
For voters who prioritize **progressive + community roots + east of river**: Owolewa, Forester, or Jenkins, with tradeoffs on insider experience.
For voters who prioritize **moderate + continuity + community service**: Chavous.
For voters who prioritize **business perspective + housing**: Hill.
For voters who prioritize **public safety**: Jackson.
For voters who prioritize **education**: Davis or Raymond.

### Ranked-choice voting note

Because this is RCV, the tool should help users build a ranking of 2 to 4 candidates rather than choose just one. The framework matrix above shows which candidates cluster together on which dimensions, which can guide secondary rankings.
